# Before Day 2: Your Checklist

Three things must happen before you walk in on Day 2: **accounts**, **software**, and **content**.
The total active time is under an hour, but the GitHub Education approval can take days to come back, so start early.
On Day 2 you will publish a real website to the internet, and this checklist makes sure nothing slows you down that morning.

## 1. Accounts (start this at least a week early)

### Create a GitHub account

If you already have a GitHub account you like, skip to the Education application below.

1. Go to [github.com](https://github.com/) and click **Sign up**.
2. Follow the prompts: enter an email, create a password, and pick a username.
   You can also use the **Continue with Google** option if you prefer.
3. Verify your email when GitHub sends you a code.
   You should now be able to sign in and see your (empty) GitHub home page.

GitHub requires you to be at least 13 years old, so everyone in this program qualifies.

> ⚠️ **Important:** your username becomes your website address.
> On Day 2 your site goes live at `https://YOUR-USERNAME.github.io`, and that address is public and hard to change later.
> Pick something you would be happy to put on a college application, like a clean version of your name.
> Username rules: letters, numbers, and hyphens only; no starting, ending, or double hyphens; 39 characters max.

### Apply to GitHub Education

GitHub Education gives verified students the free **Copilot Student** plan: unlimited code completions plus a monthly allowance of AI credits.
That is the AI assistant you will use to build your website, so it is worth applying now.

Follow the full walkthrough in [01_apply_github_education.md](01_apply_github_education.md).
It covers what proof of enrollment to prepare and how to avoid the most common rejections.

> 💡 **Tip:** the application checks your location against your school's campus, so applying while physically at school is the most reliable route.
> School out for the summer? Apply from home with your clearest proof document (like a recent transcript), and reapply if the location check complains.
> Guide 01 explains the details.

Verification usually takes about 2 to 3 business days, and the benefits can take a few more days to show up after that.
At busy times of year it can take up to two weeks.
That is why this section says "at least a week early."

### If approval has not arrived by Day 2, you are still fine

You can do everything on Day 2 with **Copilot Free**, which needs no application at all.
It has monthly limits, so we will teach you how to spend its requests wisely, and the class includes a starter template so you do not waste requests on boilerplate.
[01_apply_github_education.md](01_apply_github_education.md) explains the fallback.
Publishing your website is free for every GitHub account, so a pending application never blocks you from going live.

## 2. Software on your laptop

Set these up on the laptop you will bring to Day 2.
The full step-by-step instructions for Windows and macOS are in [02_setup_vscode_and_copilot.md](02_setup_vscode_and_copilot.md).
Here is the short version of what you need:

1. **VS Code**, the editor we will work in all day.
2. **Git**, the tool that sends your website files to GitHub.
   On Windows this is a separate install; on macOS your computer will offer to install it the first time you need it.
3. **Copilot sign-in**: open VS Code and sign in with your GitHub account so the AI features work.
   No extension install is needed; Copilot is built into VS Code.
4. **The Live Preview extension** for VS Code, which shows your web page inside the editor while you edit it.
5. **Node.js (optional)**, only needed if you install the Copilot CLI (a version of Copilot that runs in the terminal) the npm way.
   The recommended winget and Homebrew installs in [02_setup_vscode_and_copilot.md](02_setup_vscode_and_copilot.md) do not need it.

Test that VS Code opens and Copilot responds before Day 2.
Doing this on home Wi-Fi beats doing it on a crowded classroom network.

> 💡 **Tip:** bring the same laptop you set up, fully charged, and pack your charger.
> It is a full day of building.

## 3. Collect your content

Your website needs something to say.
Gather these before Day 2 and put them in one folder on your laptop so you can drop them straight into your site:

1. **A short bio, 2 to 3 sentences.**
   This becomes the intro at the top of your site, so a draft now saves you from writer's block later.
2. **A headshot or avatar image you are allowed to share publicly.**
   Ask a parent to approve the photo first, or skip the photo and use an illustration or avatar instead.
   A face is not required for a great site.
3. **A resume PDF, if you have one (optional).**
   Some students like a "Download my resume" button, and it is an easy win if the file is ready.
4. **2 to 4 "things I have built or done" items, each with 1 or 2 sentences.**
   School projects, clubs, sports, volunteering, and yes, this internship all count.
   These become your projects section, which is the part visitors actually read.
5. **Links you want to share.**
   At minimum your GitHub profile, since your site will live there anyway.
6. **2 or 3 websites you think look cool.**
   You will use them as style inspiration when you tell Copilot what look you want.
7. **Favorite colors, fonts, or vibe words.**
   Minimal? Playful? Neon? Knowing your vibe makes your prompts specific, and specific prompts get better results.

## 4. Privacy check (seriously, read this)

Your site will be on the **public internet**, indexed by search engines, visible to anyone forever.
That is exciting, and it is also why you need to think before you publish.

Never put these on your site:

- Your home address.
- Your phone number.
- Your birthday.
- Your school schedule or anything that says where you will be and when.
- Anything else you would not hand to a stranger on the street.

Your first name, or first name plus last initial, is enough to make the site feel like yours.
Photos are optional, and an avatar works just as well.

> ⚠️ **Important:** if you are unsure whether something is safe to publish, ask a parent or the instructors before it goes live.
> It is much easier to leave something off than to remove it from the internet later.
